Welcome to my personal portfolio website - still under development! My name is William Pudupakkam, and I'm a second-year Mathematical Physics major at the University of Waterloo.

Back when I was a little kid, I remember telling my parents, “when I grow up, I want to be a race car”. To my past-self's disappointment, this ambitious dream did not pan out. However, I went with the next best thing: pursuing my newfound passion for mathematics, physics, and computer science! I started becoming deeply interested in mathematics around high school, and taking physics in grades 11 & 12 instantly hooked me. This ultimately led me to major in Mathematical Physics as well as minor in Computing for my undergrad, a choice which I have not regretted since.

So far, I've solidified my foundational knowledge in physics with courses such as Mechanics, Waves/Electricity/Magnetism (along with their respective labs), as well as Modern Physics, Quantum Physics, and Computational Physics. I have an extensive mathematical background, with Calculus 1-4, Linear Algebra 1-2, Differential Equations, and Probability. A few of these courses carried usage of Python as well, giving me a familiarity with modules such as Numpy, Matplotlib, Scipy, Sympy, and more.

My general interest in computer science has also led me to pick up a multitude of new skills. For example, the development of this website is a product of my HTML, CSS and Javascript knowledge (side note: this website was made entirely from scratch; no templates). I’ve also had my eye on iOS development, leading me to pick up Apple’s Swift language. Other languages I know include Python, R, and MATLAB, which have had their uses both among my academic course load and for personal projects.

Outside my studies I enjoy playing piano to relax. I have a deep interest in music theory, and in fact am working on a project using Python that generates unique musical melodies that sound “nice” (see the projects section below for more info).

Sudoku Solver

Created a function in Python that solves Sudoku puzzles. The algorithm currently is a brute-force approach that checks every single box to see if there are any numbers that can be placed, and it repeats this process from the start every time until every box has a number. This was my first attempt at it, and I am planning on working out a much more optimized approach.
Block Collision Simulation

An interesting result in mathematics is the number of collisions two blocks make when the mass of one block is 100^n times the mass of the other. I created a simulation in Python to demonstrate that as you increase n, the number of collisions approaches the digits of Pi (i.e. there will be 3, then 31, then 314, etc. collisions as n increases).
Galton Board Simulation

One thing that really interested me in my probability course was the result that certain probability distributions can be approximated by the normal distribution for a high value of n. To prove this, I created a simulation of a Galton Board in Python that allows you to vary n (the number of balls being dropped), and in turn should produce a histogram in the shape of a normal distribution.
Linear Algebra Tools

Sometimes linear algebra calculations can get pretty tedius (i.e. finding an orthonormal basis), so I created various Python functions that carry out these processes for me.
Musical Composer

My passions for computer science and music theory collide - developing a function that takes in a root note (A, B, C, etc.) and outputs a unique musical melody using knowledge of music theory. This function will not simply generate random notes, but take into account basic concepts of music theory such as intervals, harmony, chord progressions, and more.
